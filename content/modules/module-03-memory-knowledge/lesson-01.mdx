Alzheimer's disease is a progressive brain disorder that affects memory and thinking skills. It is the most common cause of dementia — a general term for memory loss and cognitive decline severe enough to interfere with daily life. In this lesson we explore the scale of the crisis, why early detection matters, and how machine learning on MRI scans can help.

## The Scale of the Crisis

Alzheimer's disease is not just a personal tragedy — it is a public health and economic emergency. The disease is progressive and irreversible, gradually destroying memory, thinking skills, and eventually the ability to carry out simple tasks.

<Callout type="key">
One in three seniors dies with Alzheimer's or another dementia. It kills more than breast cancer and prostate cancer combined.
</Callout>

The economic toll is staggering. If Alzheimer's were a country, its $1 trillion annual cost would make it the 18th largest economy in the world — on par with Canada and Indonesia.

## The Case for Early Detection

Only 1 in 4 people with dementia receives a formal diagnosis. Without a diagnosis, patients cannot access treatment, care planning, or support services. Earlier identification also reduces long-term healthcare costs by enabling management before the disease reaches its most resource-intensive stages.

<Callout type="warning">
The detection gap is not a medical inevitability — it is a systems problem. Most cases go undiagnosed because current screening tools are too slow, too expensive, or unavailable outside specialist centres.
</Callout>

Machine learning applied to MRI scans offers a path to faster, cheaper, and more accessible screening. Structural differences in the brain — visible in cross-sectional MRI images — correlate strongly with disease stage, making this a tractable computer vision problem.

## The Dataset and Next Steps

The data used in this project comes from the OASIS dataset — MRI scans of both healthy and Alzheimer's patients, covering four classification categories: Non Demented, Very Early Demented, Mild Demented, and Moderately Demented. Images show cross-sections of the brain and capture structural differences across disease stages.

<Callout type="tip">
The OASIS dataset was created by Dr Randy Buckner at the Howard Hughes Medical Institute (HHMI) at Harvard University, in collaboration with the Washington University Alzheimer's Disease Research Center. Access it at [oasis-brains.org](https://www.oasis-brains.org/#dataAccess).
</Callout>

The modelling pipeline will follow three steps: preprocessing images (rescaling, normalising pixel values, standardising sizes), extracting features through convolutional layers of a deep learning model, and training and validating the model with standard evaluation metrics.
